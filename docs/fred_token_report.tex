\title{Special Tokens versus Plain Labels for Fredholm Integral Equations:\\
\large A Short Report on Fine-Tuning a 360M Model, with a Solver-Routing Follow-Up}
\author{Fred-LLM project}
\date{12 July 2026}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We add 15 special tokens to SmolLM2-360M-Instruct: six that mark the parts of
a Fredholm integral equation in the prompt, seven that commit the model to a
solution type with its first generated token, and two that delimit the answer.
The idea follows the tool-token literature (ToolkenGPT, Breeze-2): make the
model's first output token a classification decision. We fine-tune the full
model on Apple-silicon MLX with 11{,}100 equations generated by this
repository's augmentation pipeline, and compare against the same model
fine-tuned on the same equations written as plain text. The plain model wins,
0.505 to 0.370 on the 100-equation test set, and both beat every GPT baseline
we have (best: gpt-5.4 agentic at 0.384). A second experiment reuses the type
decision as a routing signal: when the model declares an equation solvable, a
deterministic SymPy degenerate-kernel solver solves it and its answer replaces
the model's. This raises exact-symbolic accuracy from 0.23 to 0.60 without any
retraining and lifts the plain model to 0.582. The solver without a router
scores 0.359, because it returns solutions for equations that have none. The
router earns its keep by declining to call; the special tokens never earn
theirs, since plain text labels route equally well.
\end{abstract}

\section{Problem and test set}
A Fredholm equation of the second kind has the form
\[
u(x) - \lambda \int_a^b K(x,t)\,u(t)\,dt = f(x).
\]
The benchmark is the repository's \texttt{test\_100\_v2} set: 100 equations
across seven solution types (30 exact symbolic, 15 approximate-coefficient, 15
with no solution, 10 series, 10 discrete-point, 10 solution-family, 10
ill-posed first-kind). The evaluator scores the three last-named types by
decision alone: an item with no solution is correct if the model says
``no solution,'' an ill-posed item is correct if the model labels it
\texttt{regularized}, and a family item is correct if the answer matches the
family form. So 35 of 100 points require classification rather than solving.
The GPT baselines lose most of these points; they produce a confident
closed-form answer where none exists.

\section{Token scheme}
The input tokens wrap the equation,
\begin{center}
\texttt{<|fred|> <|kernel|> K <|lambda|> $\lambda$ <|rhs|> f <|domain|> a b <|solve|>},
\end{center}
and the completion begins with one of seven decision tokens
(\texttt{<|T\_exact\_symbolic|>}, \texttt{<|T\_none|>}, and so on), followed by
\texttt{<|sol|>}, the solution in LaTeX, and \texttt{<|end|>}. Expression
bodies keep the pretrained tokenizer's own encoding; the new tokens only mark
structure and control. Each new embedding row is initialized to the mean of
the subword embeddings of a short description of that token, following the
grounded-initialization results on models of this size. SmolLM2 ties its input
and output embeddings, so one matrix is resized (49{,}152 to 49{,}216 rows,
padded to a multiple of 64).

The control condition uses no new tokens. The same equations are written as a
short natural-language prompt, and the completion is the repository's existing
plain format (\texttt{SOLUTION:~...}, \texttt{HAS\_SOLUTION:~yes/no},
\texttt{SOLUTION\_TYPE:~...}). Everything else, including the optimizer
settings, the data split, and the evaluation, is identical.

\section{Data and training}
The training set contains 11{,}100 equations produced by the repository's own
augmentation strategies, with type counts chosen to give the rare types
hundreds to thousands of examples each. Any base equation whose $\lambda$
matches a test item (to six decimals) is excluded. All expressions are
rendered with floats rounded to four significant figures, in training and at
test time. Examples longer than 1{,}000 tokens (about 1.8\%) are dropped.

Training is a full fine-tune on a 16\,GB M1 Pro under MLX, batch size 4,
prompt tokens masked from the loss. Three facts cost us most of the wall-clock
time and none of them appear in the papers we followed. First, the
\texttt{mlx-lm} LoRA command line never trains the embedding matrix, even in
full fine-tuning mode with all layers selected; the new token rows stay at
their initialization, and the model cannot learn to emit them while the
validation loss still looks reasonable, because body tokens dominate the loss.
We had to write a custom training loop that unfreezes everything. Second,
bf16 full fine-tuning at learning rate $10^{-5}$ diverged twice, once to a
pinned loss of 10.5 and once to NaN; gradient-norm clipping at 1.0 plus a drop
to $5\times10^{-6}$ held. Third, one decision token sits among roughly forty
easy body tokens, so its gradient is diluted forty to one. After 2{,}500
stable steps the model chose the correct first token only 30\% of the time.
Weighting special-token positions ten times higher in the cross-entropy
raised that to 51\% on validation.

Both fine-tuned 360M models beat every GPT run, and the margin comes from the
decision types: the fine-tuned models answer ``no solution'' and ``requires
regularization'' when the equation calls for it, and the GPT models almost
never do (regularized at 0.0 to 0.2 against our 0.9 to 1.0). Within the pair,
the special tokens lose to plain labels, 0.370 to 0.505. Solver routing then
adds more than either training choice: exact-symbolic accuracy goes from
0.185 and 0.233 to 0.600 for both routed models, with no retraining.

\section{Why plain labels beat special tokens}
The ablation isolates the token scheme; base model, data, optimizer, and
evaluator are shared.

A type decision is an output label, and a label does not need its own token.
The plain model writes \texttt{SOLUTION\_TYPE: regularized} using words whose
embeddings carry full pretraining knowledge, and it classifies better (none
0.533 against 0.400). The tool-token papers get value from special tokens
because a token there must trigger machinery mid-generation; nothing in our
v1 task needs triggering.

Fifteen new embedding rows must be learned from about 10k examples. The
description-mean initialization helps, but the plain model has zero new
parameters, and the difference shows.

The ten-times loss weighting that made the decision tokens fire also starved
the body tokens. The clearest symptom is the approximate-coefficient
column, which is scored on the solution body: 0.308 with special tokens
against 0.733 with plain labels. We bought decision accuracy with solution
accuracy and the trade lost points on net.

One caveat cuts across all of this: our models train on the same augmentation
pipeline that generated the test set, so they are in-distribution by
construction, while the GPT models run zero-shot. The comparison between the
two 360M variants is clean; the comparison against the GPTs favors us and
should be read with that in mind. All results are single runs on 100 items,
so differences under about five points are noise.

\section{The type decision as a routing signal}
The second experiment gives the decision a job. If the kernel is degenerate,
$K(x,t)=\sum_i g_i(x)h_i(t)$, the equation reduces to a small linear system:
with $A_{ij}=\int_a^b h_i g_j\,dt$ and $b_i=\int_a^b h_i f\,dt$, solve
$(I-\lambda A)c=b$ and read off $u = f + \lambda\sum_j c_j g_j$. This is a
deterministic SymPy procedure, and 59 of the 100 test kernels admit it. The
harness runs it whenever the fine-tuned model declares the equation solvable
(exact-symbolic, approximate-coefficient, or series) and splices the solver's
answer into the model's output. The model never generates solver arguments;
the equation is already in the prompt, so the decision alone routes. No
retraining or regeneration was needed; we re-scored the stored v1 outputs.

Routing works, and the direction of the failure without it is instructive.
The solver called on every equation reaches 0.833 on exact-symbolic items but
0.359 overall, because it also returns a ``solution'' for the 25 items that
have none or are ill-posed. The routed models keep those points (the plain
router holds none at 0.533 and regularized at 1.0) and reach 0.582. In strict
terms the router is worth 25 points over the raw tool. The two routers
capture the same 0.600 on exact-symbolic items, against the solver's ceiling
of 0.833; the missing seven items are ones the models misclassified as
unsolvable types. Plain labels route as well as special tokens, so the v1
conclusion stands in v2.

Two engineering notes for reproduction. Symbolic determinants over
unevaluated constants such as $e^{122}\!\cdot\mathrm{erf}(\dots)$ do not
terminate in practice; we convert the linear-system entries to floats before
any matrix work. And because one LaTeX parser (Math-Verify) silently drops
terms on some inputs, the harness parses each equation with two independent
parsers and refuses to solve when they disagree numerically (12 of 200
fields).

\section{Limitations}
The remaining gaps are concentrated where the solver does not apply.
Approximate-coefficient kernels are mostly non-separable (weakly singular or
oscillatory), and numeric quadrature recovers only part of them. Series items
stay at zero for every configuration: the winnable ones have non-separable
kernels, and where the solver does apply, its exact answer differs from the
truncated-series ground truth by more than the 1\% tolerance. Detection of
no-solution items tops out at 0.533; some of those cases need spectral
reasoning that surface features cannot supply. We tested one model size, one
seed, and one benchmark whose ground truth comes from the same generator as
our training data. The argument-generating form of the tool-token loop, where
the model emits solver parameters mid-generation and the solver's output is
masked from the loss, remains untested and is the natural next step for the
non-separable cases.
